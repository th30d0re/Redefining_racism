# Commonwealth v. David E. Canjura

**Citation:** SJC-13432 (Mass. Supreme Judicial Court, 2023-2024)
**Decided:** August 27, 2024
**County:** Suffolk

---

## Syllabus & Case Summary

The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court held that the Commonwealth's statutory ban on carrying switchblade knives (G.L. c. 269, § 10(b)) violates the Second Amendment, as interpreted by the U.S. Supreme Court in New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen (2022).

### Background
- Defendant was arrested in Boston in July 2020 and found with a spring-assisted knife (switchblade).
- Charged under G.L. c. 269, § 10(b) (dangerous weapon) and assault/battery on a family member.
- Defendant moved to dismiss, arguing the switchblade ban violates the Second Amendment.
- Trial judge denied the motion; defendant admitted to sufficient facts but reserved appeal.
- Assault charge was dismissed; Supreme Judicial Court granted direct appellate review.

### Legal Analysis
- The Second Amendment protects the right to keep and bear "arms," not limited to firearms.
- Folding pocketknives (including switchblades) were commonly possessed for lawful purposes at the time of the founding.
- Switchblades fit the historical definition of "arms" and are presumptively protected.
- The Commonwealth failed to show a historical tradition of regulating folding pocketknives or switchblades at the time of the Second or Fourteenth Amendment ratification.
- Modern switchblade bans (1950s-60s) are too recent to establish constitutional tradition under Bruen.
- Switchblades are in "common use" today and not "dangerous and unusual" weapons outside Second Amendment protection.

### Holding
- G.L. c. 269, § 10(b) is unconstitutional as applied to switchblade knives.
- Defendant's conviction is vacated; judgment for the defendant on the switchblade charge.

---

## Key Points
- Second Amendment covers all bearable arms, including knives.
- No historical tradition justifies a categorical ban on switchblades.
- Switchblades are widely owned and accepted for lawful self-defense.
- Statute invalidated only as to switchblade knives; other portions remain intact.
