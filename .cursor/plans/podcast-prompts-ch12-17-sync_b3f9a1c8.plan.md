### ep15-boundary

A new Falsifiability Appendix (Appendix D) explicitly states the conditions under which both the Concession Theorem and the Haitian Theorem would be falsified — the book holds itself to an empirical standard.

### ep15-zimbabwe
Add to the Haitian Theorem bullet, after the Taiping Rebellion entry: **Zimbabwe (1980–2008): The "locally" qualifier under stress.** Robert Mugabe's ZANU-PF achieved kinetic liberation from Rhodesian white-minority extraction in 1980. $\max = 0$ locally for the colonial extraction architecture — confirmed. But the new governing elite progressively recompiled a new extraction kernel, extracting from the same population through different mechanisms. The Haitian Theorem's qualifier "locally" is load-bearing: kinetic action can dismantle *a specific extraction architecture* but does not automatically prevent the installation of a successor hierarchy. The theorem is about the mechanism of structural break, not a guarantee of permanent liberation. Zimbabwe is the scope-stress case that makes the qualifier precise.

## Episode 16 — 3 inserts (Chapter 13 hardening)

### ep16-global-durability
Add as new beat after the International 5-Tier Hierarchy section: **The Global Durability Objection — China, OPEC, and the Asian Tigers.** Three apparent counter-cases to the Imperial Core Theorem are the most frequently cited: China's rise as a global economic power, the 1973 OPEC embargo, and the Asian Tiger economies (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong). Apply the framework's two-part $\Delta\max = 0$ test: (1) Did the case achieve local $\max = 0$ (dismantlement of the Atlantic extraction architecture)? (2) Did it alter the Imperial Core Theorem's global extraction kernel? China's rise represents a renegotiation of extraction terms within the global system, not dismantlement — China has constructed its own peripheral extraction architecture in Africa and Latin America that replicates the 5-Tier template. The 1973 OPEC embargo achieved temporary leverage ($P_{\text{debt}}$ partial reversal) but did not prevent re-subordination — the petrodollar system, IMF conditionality, and dollar weaponization rebuilt the extraction gradient within a decade. The Asian Tigers achieved development through integration into the imperial supply chain (favorable export access granted by the U.S. during the Cold War as a geopolitical subsidy) — not through disruption of the core. None of the three cases passes both tests. They are scope-boundary negotiations, not kernel breaks.

### ep16-scope
Add after the Global Durability Objection beat: **Scope Condition: The Atlantic-Origin Constraint.** The Imperial Core Theorem and the International 5-Tier Hierarchy are explicitly bounded to Atlantic-origin extraction architecture and its institutional successors (IMF, World Bank, WTO, dollar hegemony, NATO). Non-Western imperial systems — the Ottoman Empire, the Mongol Khanates, the Mughal Empire — operated extraction architectures with different structural logics and are outside the framework's scope. The constraint matters for analytical precision: the framework claims that *Atlantic-origin* extraction is uniquely characterized by the racial partition ($I_{\text{buffer}}$ vs. $O_{\text{racialized}}$), the psychological wage ($\psi$), and the kinetic-guarantee asymmetry that makes the Concession Theorem's absorption mechanism self-repairing. Non-Atlantic systems may share some structural features but lack the specific partition variable that makes this framework's prescription necessary.

The window between now and $t_{\text{automation}}$ is finite; its exact length is the open question, not its existence.

The political distinction (foreign/domestic) does not change the graph-theoretic bottleneck. The procurement timeline as the state's response to its own defeat model remains the correct framing — but the framing is inference from structural logic, not literal self-confession.
