## Revision 1 — Domestic Portugal Evidence Gap for the Psychological Wage
**Location:** Section "The Original Implicit Contract and the Psychological Wage," around line 881.

**Current text (line 881):**
> "In its inaugural Portuguese deployment, $\psi_m = 0$: the Elite pacified their working class without spending a single coin..."

**Problem:** The book claims Portuguese peasants received a psychological wage but the enslaved population was geographically separated (Madeira, Brazil), so the "floor of humanity" status was rarely visible or tested domestically. Grok (and likely peer reviewers) will press on this.

**Fix:** After the `$\psi_m = 0$` sentence, add a brief paragraph that:
- Acknowledges the geographic separation explicitly (the enslaved population was offshore, not in Lisbon streets)
- Provides the domestic transmission mechanism: the Casa dos Escravos legal infrastructure, royal proclamations, and Zurara's chronicle circulated inside Portugal as the primary vehicle. The psychological wage was delivered through *legal and narrative status* rather than visible proximity — Portuguese peasants did not need to see enslaved Africans daily; they needed only to know they were legally categorized as a different order of being.
- Notes that this is consistent with the pattern in later deployments: the psychological wage does not require physical co-presence; it requires only that the legal partition be legible to the In-group.

## Revision 2 — Kidnapping-to-Trade Transition and African Kingdom Agency
**Location:** Subsection "The Kidnapping Operations: What Zurara Actually Documented," lines 852–853.

**Current text (line 852):**
> "The transition from raiding to 'trading' was itself a function of the arms asymmetry documented in Chapter~\ref{ch:kinetic}."

**Problem:** The book frames the shift as entirely Portuguese-driven (arms asymmetry forced a logistics pivot), leaving African kingdoms' agency as a gap that critics can exploit.

**Fix:** Expand the transition sentence into 2–3 sentences that:
- Acknowledge that coastal and interior African kingdoms (Dahomey, Asante, Kongo) actively participated in the trade by supplying war captives
- Immediately turn this into a strengthening argument: this participation does not undermine the moral hack thesis — it demonstrates the system's *modularity*. The Portuguese algorithm did not require total coercion everywhere; it could interface with pre-existing African elite extraction hierarchies by offering a sufficiently attractive exchange rate (firearms, textiles, metal goods). The algorithm was exportable precisely because it could recruit local elites rather than depending solely on Portuguese military force.
- This reframes "African agency" from a critique of the book into *evidence for* the framework's central claim about elite extraction being a general-purpose engine.

## Revision 3 — Firmin and Algorithm Resilience (Logical Tightening)
**Location:** Subsection "The Counter-Signal: Anténor Firmin," lines 1104–1106.

**Current text:**
> "This is not an indictment of Firmin---it is a structural observation about the algorithm's resilience. The extraction kernel does not require all priors to be enforced at once..."

**Problem:** The logical step — "Firmin was sexist, therefore the algorithm is resilient" — is circular. The sexism is being used to *illustrate* a conclusion that isn't independently established.

**Fix:** Rewrite the concluding framing so the claim is properly grounded:
- The algorithm's resilience is demonstrated not *by* Firmin's sexism but by the structural observation that variable-based sorting had penetrated the *epistemological infrastructure* so thoroughly that even rigorous empirical critics operated within some of its partitions.
- Firmin's case is then an *instance* of this (the most striking instance because of who he was), not the *proof* of it.
- Essentially: reverse the logical order — state the general mechanism first, then cite Firmin as the sharpest historical illustration.
