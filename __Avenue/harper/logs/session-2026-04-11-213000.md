## Challenges Encountered

1. **Literature silos**: Environmental epidemiology and IPV scholarship operate as completely separate disciplines — different journals, conferences, and methodological traditions. Nobody bridges them.
2. **Raw counts vs. rates**: The BJS data provides raw IPH counts, not per-capita rates. Black female population was growing during 1976–2005, so the raw count plateau actually represents a RATE plateau or slower decline — even more consistent with the lead hypothesis.
3. **Underreporting confound**: The very communities most affected by lead had the strongest reasons NOT to report IPV. The IPH data (requiring a dead body) captures only the lethal tip of a massive unreported iceberg.
4. **Carceral feedback loop**: Mass incarceration made reporting even more fraught — Black women had to weigh partner's safety against their own, effectively silencing the measurement of non-lethal IPV.
5. **Confounding variables**: Same neighborhoods had compounding stressors (poverty, unemployment, deindustrialization, drug markets). The boyfriend/girlfriend IPH data helps isolate the lead variable because it shows a pattern inconsistent with other explanations (social programs should have REDUCED this category, not increased it).
6. **Flint as prospective experiment**: The Flint water crisis (2014) children won't reach peak offending age until ~2030–2040, so the natural experiment hasn't matured yet.
