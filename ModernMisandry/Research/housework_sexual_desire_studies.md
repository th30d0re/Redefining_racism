# Academic Studies on Housework Division and Sexual Desire

This document gathers the real studies referenced in the social media post and clarifies how each one connects to the claim that unequal household labor is associated with lower sexual desire in women. It also provides direct links where possible and details any mismatches between the meme's citations and the actual research.


## 1. Ciciolla, L., & Luthar, S. (2019). *Invisible Household Labor and Ramifications for Adjustment: Mothers as Captains of Households*. Sex Roles.
**Link:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8223758/

### Summary
This study focuses on "invisible labor" which includes planning, scheduling, and emotional management of the household. Women who carry the majority of these tasks report higher mental load, more stress, and lower relational fulfillment.

### Relevance to sexual desire
Although the paper does not explicitly study sexual frequency or libido, it demonstrates that emotional exhaustion and stress undermine relationship satisfaction… circumstances that reliably correlate with diminished interest in intimacy.


## 2. Carlson, D. L., Hanson, S., & Fitzroy, A. (2016). *The Gendered Division of Housework and Couples' Sexual Relationships: A Re-examination*. Journal of Marriage and Family.
**Link:** https://www.jstor.org/stable/24734766
**Alternate link:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/303534154

### Summary
This well known study examines heterosexual couples and the relationship between household labor division and sexual frequency. It finds that couples who share housework in a collaborative pattern tend to have higher sexual frequency than couples where chores follow highly gendered traditions.

### Relevance to sexual desire
This work supports the general idea that equitable household labor strengthens emotional closeness and can increase sexual connection. The more uneven the division… especially when women shoulder most chores… the more sexual frequency tends to drop.

### Note on citation mismatch
The meme attributes this to the *Journal of Sex Research*, but the actual publication is *Journal of Marriage and Family*.


## 3. Harris, C. R., et al. (2022). *Gender Inequities in Household Labor Predict Lower Sexual Desire in Women Partnered with Men*.
**Link:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9483460/

### Summary
This study directly examines sexual desire among women in heterosexual relationships and compares it against their perceived share of housework. The more inequitable the division, the more likely women are to report lower desire.

### Relevance
This is the strongest empirical confirmation of the meme's claim. It demonstrates a measurable relationship between unequal domestic labor and reduced libido.


## 4. Socius: Sociological Research for a Dynamic World (2020) – Relevant Work by Carlson, Miller, & Rudd
**Link:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2378023120924805

### Summary
This paper focuses on how communication and fairness around household labor affects relationship satisfaction. It does not measure sexual desire directly, but it confirms that perceived unfairness reduces emotional connection.

### Relevance
Lower relationship satisfaction correlates with lower sexual desire in numerous other studies, making this an indirect but meaningful contributor.

### Note on citation mismatch
The meme references “Kluwer, E. S. Socius, 2020.” No such study appears in Socius under that author and year. The closest match is the Carlson et al. paper above.


# Overall Conclusions
Across multiple studies in psychology and sociology, researchers observe a consistent pattern:

1. When women perform a disproportionate share of physical and mental labor at home… stress, resentment, and emotional distance increase.
2. These variables correlate strongly with diminished sexual desire, reduced sexual frequency, or lower relationship satisfaction.
3. Collaborative or fair division of labor tends to strengthen intimacy, emotional security, and sexual connection.

The meme exaggerates the simplicity of the relationship, but its core idea mirrors several peer reviewed findings.
