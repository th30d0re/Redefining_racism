Involuntary Celibacy in Long-Term Relationships: Psychological, Relational, and Physical Health Effects

Introduction: Involuntary celibacy in a romantic relationship refers to a scenario where one partner desires sexual intimacy but the other partner withholds sex, leading to a sexless or near-sexless relationship. This situation is not uncommon: by some estimates, roughly one in seven marriages has little to no sexual activity (about 7% of married U.S. adults report no sex in the past year, and 14–15% have sex very infrequently) ￼. Unlike mutually agreed celibacy, involuntary celibacy often causes significant distress because the deprived partner’s needs are unmet. Below, we review peer-reviewed research on the psychological, relational, and physical health outcomes of this phenomenon, focusing on long-term partnerships (marriages, cohabiting couples) and noting relevant findings from shorter relationships. We include data across genders, as both men and women (and couples of all orientations) can be affected.

Psychological Effects of Involuntary Celibacy

When one partner chronically withholds sex, the other partner typically experiences a range of negative psychological outcomes. Research and clinical studies have documented the following key effects:
	•	Depression and Emotional Distress: Prolonged sexual rejection is linked to depressed mood, sadness, and even clinical depression. In a qualitative study of 77 involuntarily celibate spouses (ages 18–65, ~50% men, 50% women), participants “almost universally” reported frustration, feelings of rejection, and depression as a result of the sexlessness. One middle-aged husband described the situation as “deleterious… I dwell on sexual thoughts and fantasies. I am depressed. My professional life is impacted” ￼. These findings align with survey research showing that being sexually inactive (when one wishes otherwise) correlates with poorer mental health, including greater unhappiness and psychological distress. Notably, lack of physical intimacy means missing the mood-enhancing benefits of sex – studies find that regular sexual activity is associated with lower rates of depression and greater life satisfaction.
	•	Anxiety and Self-Esteem Issues: Involuntary celibacy can also erode self-esteem and induce anxiety about one’s desirability. Donnelly et al. (2001) interviewed individuals who had gone 6+ months without sex and found pervasive feelings of worthlessness and inadequacy. For example, one man reported “no woman has ever wanted to be sexual with me… It is tremendously damaging to my self-esteem… I think about it every day, every hour”. Partners in sexless marriages often internalize the rejection, worrying “maybe it’s me” – this can manifest as performance anxiety or body image concerns. In the 77-couple study, many spouses (both genders) said the lack of sex made them feel unattractive, unwanted, and anxious that the problem might never be resolved. Such chronic anxiety can create a vicious cycle, further undermining confidence in initiating intimacy.
	•	Resentment, Anger, and Frustration: Emotional fallout frequently includes resentment toward the withholding partner. About one-third of involuntarily celibate individuals in one survey explicitly voiced dissatisfaction, frustration or anger about the lack of sex. These negative emotions were present “regardless of partnership status” – whether in a marriage or single, people deprived of desired sex felt upset. In couples, resentment can be directed at the partner who refuses intimacy, sometimes simmering under the surface and emerging as irritability or hostility in unrelated conflicts. One wife in a sexless marriage confessed to feeling “resentful… hurt, tears… missing intimacy. Forever.”. This kind of chronic frustration can also co-occur with anxiety about the relationship’s future, creating emotional turmoil.
	•	Loneliness and Low Life Satisfaction: Counterintuitively, a person can feel lonely within a marriage when physical affection is absent. Several studies note that lack of sexual and emotional intimacy leaves partners feeling isolated and disconnected, even while living together. Involuntarily celibate individuals often report a hollowness or missing piece in their lives – their relationship may function superficially, but without the bond of physical intimacy they feel “alone” in their experience. Over time, this can erode overall life satisfaction. For instance, a longitudinal study of older couples found that those who ceased sexual activity or felt unhappy with their sexual frequency had significantly lower overall happiness and higher psychological distress than those with a satisfying sex life. Simply put, an unsatisfying sex life can cast a shadow over one’s mental well-being.

It’s important to note that these psychological effects are observed across genders. Both men and women experience depression, low self-esteem, and resentment when their intimate needs go chronically unfulfilled. That said, some research hints at gender differences in coping: for example, one study of young heterosexual couples found that a large desire gap predicted significantly lower overall relationship satisfaction for men, whereas for women it mainly predicted lower sexual satisfaction (women tended to separate the sexual from the relational to a greater extent). In other words, men might more directly equate a sexless marriage with an unhappy marriage, while women might report “I love my partner, but I’m unhappy with our sex life.” Nonetheless, the emotional pain of involuntary celibacy clearly affects people of any gender.

Relational and Interpersonal Outcomes

Prolonged sexual withholding can strain the relationship itself, leading to various interpersonal and relational consequences:
	•	Communication Breakdown: Couples in this situation often struggle to communicate about sex. The rejecting partner may avoid the topic, and the frustrated partner may fear that bringing it up will lead to conflict. Over time, this creates a communication blackout around intimacy. In Donnelly’s research, many partners described attempts to initiate sex being “brutally refused,” after which “talking about sex… is out of the question.”. This avoidance erodes open communication in the marriage, not just about sex but in general – partners start walking on eggshells. The lack of honest dialogue can breed misunderstandings and emotional distance. In therapy contexts, desire discrepancy is noted as a major source of couple conflict; establishing safe, non-judgmental communication is often the first step to addressing it. Without that, small resentments fester and real issues go unresolved, weakening the partnership.
	•	Marital Dissatisfaction and Emotional Distancing: Not surprisingly, involuntary sexual abstinence tends to reduce relationship satisfaction. When one partner persistently feels rejected, both may withdraw emotionally to avoid pain or guilt. Intimacy (both physical and emotional) is considered a “basic psychological need” in long-term relationships – it fosters bonding, trust, and marital adjustment. A lack of intimacy, conversely, is “one of the most common causes of distress and collapse among couples,” associated with greater incompatibility, chronic stress between partners, and even psychological maladaptation in the relationship. Couples may remain together but become more like roommates than romantic partners. This emotional distancing was evident in qualitative accounts: e.g. a young wife lamented that her husband was “prepared to live with me like this… I can’t accept this as a way of life”. Research confirms that intimacy is strongly tied to relationship quality – one review concluded that lack of intimacy was a significant predictor of divorce, especially in later life marriages ￼. In short, a sexless partnership (when unwanted) often suffers lower relationship satisfaction and a fragile emotional connection.
	•	Conflict and Tension: The disparity in sexual desire can lead to frequent arguments or unspoken tension. The deprived partner might feel irritable or rejected, while the low-desire partner might feel pressured or inadequate. This mismatch is a well-documented issue: sexual desire discrepancy is among the top reasons couples seek therapy. Day-to-day, this can manifest in conflicts not overtly about sex – minor issues become proxy battles for the deeper frustration. Studies note that couples with unresolved desire differences report more marital conflict and strain. Additionally, if one or both partners misinterpret the situation (e.g. “they don’t love me anymore” or “they only care about sex”), it can breed mistrust and insecurity, further fueling disputes. Without intervention, a cycle of pursuit-withdrawal can develop: one partner increasingly pursues (or protests) for sex, while the other withdraws further, entrenching the conflict.
	•	Infidelity Risk: When sexual needs go unmet at home, some individuals eventually seek intimacy elsewhere. Studies have indeed found a link between sexual dissatisfaction and infidelity. In the 77-person involuntary celibacy study, about 26% of participants admitted to having an affair as a way to cope with the lack of sex. (Others sought alternative outlets like masturbation (79%) or even cybersex (13–14%) as coping strategies.) Low sexual fulfillment is often cited as a motive in marital infidelity research. Of course, not everyone in a sexless marriage will cheat – many remain faithful but unhappy. In fact, Donnelly & Burgess (2008) found that a slight majority of their sample did not pursue extramarital sex despite their frustration. Instead, they coped via communication attempts, therapy, or simply “resigned themselves to a life without sex”. Still, the temptation or risk of infidelity looms larger as the period of abstinence lengthens, particularly if the couple’s efforts to address the issue have stalled. This can further jeopardize the relationship, as actual affairs – or even suspicion of cheating – introduce new levels of hurt and distrust.
	•	Separation and Divorce: Involuntary celibacy can be a contributing factor to break-ups, especially if accompanied by the above issues (poor communication, resentment, etc.). While many people in long-term sexless relationships do stay for various reasons (children, financial ties, love for their partner outside of sex, fear of loneliness), others eventually decide to leave. Qualitative evidence shows some partners reach a breaking point where the emotional pain outweighs the benefits of staying. Lack of sexual intimacy is, as noted, a significant predictor in divorce for some couples. Even in dating relationships, a sharp incompatibility in sexual needs often leads to dissolution if unresolved. (In shorter-term relationships, partners may be less invested and thus more willing to end things if sex is a major frustration.) Indeed, studies of young adult couples indicate that large desire discrepancies can undermine relationship stability over time. In summary, while not every involuntarily celibate relationship ends in separation, this issue can drive a wedge that proves insurmountable for many couples.

Gender considerations: Both men and women can be either the high-desire or the low-desire partner – involuntary celibacy is not a gender-specific phenomenon. For instance, contrary to stereotypes, researchers note that in some heterosexual marriages the wife is the one craving more sex and the husband is withholding, whereas other times it’s vice versa. Same-sex couples are not immune either; surveys of LGBT couples find that long-term lesbian and gay relationships can also experience sexless periods for similar reasons (e.g. mismatched libido, conflict, health issues). The emotional and relational fallout (frustration, communication issues, etc.) appears broadly similar across genders and orientations, though individual coping strategies may differ. Some evidence (as mentioned) suggests men might tie sexual rejection more directly to their sense of the relationship, whereas women might compartmentalize it slightly more – but both report feeling unhappy, undesired, and distressed when stuck in a celibate partnership. Thus, all genders can suffer negative relational outcomes from prolonged involuntary celibacy in a relationship.

Physical Health Outcomes

Chronic stress and lack of sexual activity in an involuntarily celibate relationship may also impact physical health. Although this area is less studied than psychological or relational effects, existing research points to several connections:
	•	Stress Hormones and Physiological Stress: The stress of feeling rejected or chronically unsatisfied can elevate physiological stress markers. Partners in sexless marriages often report high stress, and biologically this may manifest as increased cortisol (the stress hormone) and blood pressure. Notably, regular sexual intimacy tends to buffer stress responses. In an experiment, people who had recent penile-vaginal intercourse showed significantly lower blood pressure reactivity to stressors than those abstaining or engaging only in non-coital activity. This suggests that the absence of sex might remove a potential stress-reliever. Over time, chronic stress can have cascading health effects – sustained high cortisol is linked to sleep problems, lowered immunity, and even suppressed testosterone in men. (Indeed, while short-term sexual abstinence might cause a transient uptick in testosterone, prolonged stress from involuntary abstinence could negate that benefit.) In sum, a tense, sexless home life can keep the body in “fight or flight” mode, with possible consequences like headaches, muscle tension, or other stress-related ailments.
	•	Sleep Disturbances: Satisfying sexual activity – especially orgasm – is well known to promote relaxation and better sleep, via release of hormones like oxytocin and prolactin. Couples who have sex before bed often report improved sleep quality. Conversely, lacking sexual release may contribute to poorer sleep. There isn’t direct experimental research on sexless marriages and sleep, but indirect evidence suggests a link. A large survey found about 75% of people slept better after sex/orgasm and had measurably fewer awakenings during the night. Thus, an involuntarily celibate individual might miss out on these sleep benefits. Additionally, anxiety or rumination about the relationship can keep one up at night. Participants in a sexless relationship study mentioned difficulty concentrating on anything other than sex – such preoccupation can easily extend to nighttime, with racing thoughts interfering with sleep. Over time, chronic sleep deprivation can further harm mood and health, creating a vicious cycle. (On the flip side, some people in these relationships turn to intense exercise or other activities to tire themselves out – coping mechanisms that might indirectly help sleep but don’t address the core issue.)
	•	Sexual Dysfunction: Ironically, prolonged lack of sex can itself lead to sexual dysfunctions in the long run. The adage “use it or lose it” has some truth, particularly for male sexual function. A longitudinal study of older men (55–75) found that those who had intercourse less than once a week had twice the risk of developing erectile dysfunction over the next 5 years compared to men who had sex at least weekly. Regular sexual activity seems to maintain erectile function (likely via cardiovascular and neural mechanisms), whereas sexual inactivity is a risk factor for ED. In a sexless marriage, a husband might initially have normal function, but after years of infrequency he could encounter erection difficulties when attempts are finally made – potentially exacerbating the cycle of avoidance. For women, low sexual activity can lead to decreased vaginal lubrication and elasticity, especially around menopause, possibly making intercourse uncomfortable when it does occur. In both sexes, long gaps in sexual experience can increase performance anxiety (“sexual rustiness”), further impairing function. Involuntarily celibate individuals sometimes report feeling “out of practice” or even developing aversions or fear around sex due to repeated negative experiences. All of this suggests that unintended celibacy can have concrete physiological effects on one’s sexual systems.
	•	Hormonal and Immune Changes: Sexual intimacy triggers hormonal responses (like oxytocin, dopamine) that confer health benefits such as stress reduction, bonding, and even pain relief. Lack of these positive hormonal surges might have subtle health impacts. Some small studies have noted that people who have regular sex tend to have stronger immune function (e.g. higher IgA antibodies) than those who abstain, though more research is needed. Hormonally, while definitive data are limited, one could speculate that a persistent state of sexual frustration might dysregulate certain hormones. For instance, chronic sexual frustration might keep cortisol elevated (due to stress), which over time can contribute to issues like weight gain or hypertension. It might also reduce the frequency of beneficial oxytocin release that comes with affectionate touch and orgasm – oxytocin is linked not only to bonding but also to lowering blood pressure and inflammation. Thus, involuntary celibacy could plausibly have hormonal ripple effects that affect overall health, although this remains an area for further scientific study.
	•	General Health and Longevity: Surprisingly, population research has found correlations between low sexual frequency and worse general health outcomes. A 2024 study of over 17,000 adults (20–59 years old) discovered that those who had sex fewer than 12 times per year (essentially “sexless” by that definition) had the highest rates of developing cardiovascular disease and also higher all-cause mortality, compared to those with more frequent sex ￼. As sexual frequency increased up to about 1–2 times per week, the risk of heart problems and death steadily decreased ￼. This is correlational (many factors could explain it), but it aligns with other findings that a healthy sex life often coincides with better physical health. In fact, other research has linked frequent sex to lower risk of certain cancers (prostate and breast cancer), improved heart health, and even better cognitive function. Part of this could be because a lack of sex might reflect or induce other unhealthy states – for example, depression (which is linked to heart disease), or a relationship so strained it affects one’s appetite or exercise habits. While we must be cautious about causality, it’s reasonable to say that involuntary sexual abstinence can be both a cause and consequence of health issues. The stress and negative emotions can take a toll on the body, potentially contributing to conditions like high blood pressure or weakened immunity; concurrently, certain health problems (e.g. obesity, hormonal imbalances) can reduce libido, creating a feedback loop. Regardless, the take-home message from the epidemiological data is that extremely infrequent sex is associated with poorer physical health outcomes ￼ – reinforcing the idea that sexual well-being is part of overall well-being.

As the table above illustrates, a growing body of credible research confirms that withholding sex in a long-term relationship can have serious psychological and relational repercussions, and even measurable physical health effects. Involuntarily celibate partners frequently experience depression, anxiety, anger, and low self-worth; their relationships suffer from communication breakdown, conflict, and sometimes infidelity or dissolution; and their bodies bear the stress, with potential impacts on sexual function, sleep, and overall health. These findings hold across genders – any individual deprived of intimacy they desire is likely to feel distress – and apply in marriages, cohabiting partnerships, and even dating relationships when expectations diverge.

Conclusion: In summary, sex is often called the “barometer” of a romantic relationship, and when one partner unilaterally turns off that tap, the climate tends to worsen. Peer-reviewed studies paint a consistent picture: prolonged involuntary celibacy in a partnership is rarely benign. It is associated with emotional pain (from sadness to resentment), erosion of relationship quality (less intimacy and trust), and even physiological changes (from stress chemistry to increased health risks). Of course, every couple’s situation has unique aspects – the causes of sexual withholding can range from medical issues to interpersonal conflict – and not all sexless relationships are doomed (some couples find fulfillment in other ways). However, when the celibacy is unwanted by one partner, the negative outcomes identified in research are important to acknowledge and address. Therapists often encourage couples in this predicament to seek open communication, medical help if needed, and compromises or solutions (such as sensate focus exercises, scheduling intimacy, or counseling to resolve underlying issues). The silver lining is that many couples can recover physical intimacy with effort, potentially reversing some of the ill effects. What the science makes clear is that ignoring the problem is dangerous – involuntary celibacy is not “harmless” and tends to fester. For the health of both the relationship and the individuals involved, it’s an issue best brought into the open and worked on collaboratively.
