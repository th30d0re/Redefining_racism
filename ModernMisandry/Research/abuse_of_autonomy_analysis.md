# Abuse of Autonomy
### A relational analysis of parallel patterns between EJ’s mother and Sapphire

## Introduction
This document examines a recurring dynamic observed in two different relationships: EJ’s mother and EJ’s former partner, Sapphire. While the contexts are different, the psychological architecture is remarkably similar. Both individuals exhibit a pattern where they desire authority, emotional centrality, or relational power… yet consistently avoid the responsibilities required to hold those roles. This mismatch creates instability, resentment, and asymmetric emotional labor for EJ.

The purpose of this document is to outline these patterns clearly so EJ can recognize them in future relationships and avoid becoming the stabilizing force for people who cannot sustain the roles they claim.

## Part One
## Defining abused autonomy
Abused autonomy occurs when someone seeks the benefits of independence, authority, or control… while simultaneously refusing the duties, accountability, or preparation that autonomy requires.

This results in a recurring three part cycle.

### Claiming a role
The person positions themselves as the leader, coordinator, authority, moral guide, or emotional center.

### Failing the responsibilities of that role
Because they do not plan, prepare, reflect, or reciprocate, they cannot maintain the role they claimed.

### Redirecting blame
To avoid confronting the mismatch between the role they want and the effort they put in, they project fault downward or outward, often toward people they believe have less power.

## Part Two
## EJ’s mother
### Pattern Overview
EJ’s mother attempts to occupy the role of matriarch and coordinator… yet consistently fails to do the logistical or emotional work required to sustain either leadership or autonomy.

### Illustrations from the moving situation
#### She wanted to control the move
She wanted people to pack in the way she envisioned, follow her strategies, and meet her standards… even though she did not pack the house in advance, did not organize the space, and did not create a workable staging area.

#### She did not fulfill the duties of leadership
She did not plan early, did not prepare boxes, did not clean her space sufficiently for helpers, and did not develop a workable timeline.

#### She redirected stress downward
She blamed Bianca, the Haitian helpers, the movers, her daughter, and even the quality of tape and boxes. She also implied theft and incompetence on the part of helpers while shielding herself from owning the disorganization.

This is not isolated behavior. It is a longstanding generational pattern of wanting the authority of motherhood without the structural planning that motherhood requires in adulthood.

## Part Three
## Sapphire
### Pattern Overview
Sapphire expressed the same psychological pattern but through ideology rather than logistics. Instead of using household roles or respectability hierarchies, she used feminism as a shield for emotional avoidance. Her version was an inverted hierarchy where women hold moral authority and men are treated as inherently suspect.

This structure let her dominate the emotional frame without being accountable to the relationship.

### Illustrations from the relationship
#### She wanted the role of partner
She wanted intimacy, connection, emotional centrality, and the comforts of being loved by a man.

#### She refused the responsibilities of partnership
She withheld affection, dismissed EJ’s emotional needs, and refused to participate in the work of mutuality. She framed EJ’s requests as male entitlement rather than human vulnerability.

#### She used ideology as a substitute for accountability
Her hatred of men was treated as a political principle, not an emotional bias. She treated EJ’s needs as an extension of patriarchy rather than part of a relationship. This allowed her to avoid responsibility for the harm she caused because the hierarchy she used placed EJ permanently beneath her.

Once again, this is the same architecture as EJ’s mother… only translated into political language.

## Part Four
## The shared architecture
Despite differences in context, EJ’s mother and Sapphire share structural traits.

### Role inflation
Both claim roles they cannot sustain. The matriarch who cannot organize. The partner who cannot reciprocate. The authority who cannot prepare. The feminist who cannot empathize with a male partner. The coordinator who cannot coordinate. The lover who cannot love consistently.

### Responsibility avoidance
Both avoid the duties that come with the positions they want: planning, preparation, vulnerability, reciprocity, consistency, accountability.

### Downward projection
Both displace blame onto people below them in whatever hierarchy they imagine: EJ, helpers, Haitian women, movers, male partners, anyone who cannot challenge their narrative.

### Self exoneration
Both preserve their self image at the expense of truth: using ideology or guilt to avoid introspection, using narrative rewrites to avoid shame, using emotional escalation to avoid accountability.

### Asymmetric empathy
Both expect empathy from EJ. Neither consistently gives it back. Neither recognizes EJ’s needs as equal to their own. Both treat disagreement as disrespect.

### Emotional authoritarianism
Both treat their feelings as inherently valid. Both treat EJ’s feelings as conditional, suspect, or secondary. Both try to dominate the emotional frame rather than share it.

## Part Five
## Why EJ became the stabilizing force
Because EJ was raised inside this psychology, his nervous system learned to:

• anticipate chaos
• compensate for other people’s disorganization
• build structure for people who cannot structure themselves
• provide empathy to people who withhold it
• absorb blame from people who externalize it
• stabilize relationships that should collapse naturally
• find attraction in familiar emotional architecture

This creates attraction to partners who want authority without labor… want intimacy without vulnerability… want love without responsibility.

These patterns were not random. They were inherited.

## Part Six
## The cost
EJ ends up carrying the emotional weight, doing the planning, providing the stability, being the adult, absorbing the chaos, losing autonomy, and being blamed for the fallout.

Meanwhile the other person avoids accountability, resents boundaries, misuses autonomy, and denies EJ’s humanity when confronted.

This is emotional asymmetry disguised as partnership.

## Conclusion
Both EJ’s mother and Sapphire demonstrate the same relational architecture: claiming authority without executing the responsibilities necessary to sustain it. When confronted with their own contribution to dysfunction, both redirect blame to maintain their emotional comfort. The roles they want and the effort they put in do not match, and EJ becomes the stabilizing force who pays the cost.

Recognizing this pattern allows EJ to break it. The next step is learning how to identify these dynamics early and how to choose relationships where autonomy and responsibility are shared rather than weaponized.
