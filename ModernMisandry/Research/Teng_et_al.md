Effects of Prolonged Sexual Abstinence on Health: A Gender-Based Review (2015–2025)
Introduction
Prolonged sexual abstinence – defined as refraining from sexual activity (including intercourse and often masturbation) for months or years – has been examined in recent studies for its impact on physical and mental health. Sexual activity is known to influence various physiological systems and emotional well-being, but there is no “required” amount of sex for good health, and many people lead healthy lives with little or no sexual activity . Researchers in the past decade (2015–2025) have explored how abstaining from sex affects hormone levels, immune function, sleep, stress, mood, and relationship dynamics in men and women. This report summarizes peer-reviewed findings, comparing outcomes between genders and highlighting key studies from credible academic sources.
Physical Health Effects of Prolonged Sexual Abstinence
Regular sexual activity has been associated with several physical health benefits, so extended abstinence may entail the absence of these benefits. Notably, a sexually active lifestyle tends to positively influence physical fitness, immune function, cardiovascular health, circadian rhythms, blood pressure, and stress regulation. By contrast, long-term abstinence means one might not realize such benefits, though simply not having sex is not generally harmful to health in itself . Below we examine specific physiological domains:
Hormonal Changes (Testosterone, Estrogen, Oxytocin)
Men: Short-term sexual abstinence can cause transient changes in male hormone levels. Small experimental studies have shown that a period of abstinence may elevate testosterone. For example, after 3 weeks without ejaculation, men’s baseline testosterone levels were significantly higher than before abstinence. Similarly, a frequently-cited study found men who abstained for ~7 days experienced a peak in testosterone around the 1-week mark. However, large-sample research indicates that masturbation or ejaculation has no lasting effect on baseline testosterone levels. In other words, any testosterone boost from abstinence is likely short-lived; long-term abstinence does not seem to chronically raise or lower men’s testosterone in a meaningful way.
Abstinence also withholds the hormonal surges associated with orgasm and sexual intimacy. In men, sexual climax acutely releases neurohormones like oxytocin and prolactin, which foster relaxation and satiety. Oxytocin (the “bonding” hormone) and endorphins released during sex can reduce stress and improve mood. Prolactin release after orgasm contributes to the post-sex sense of calm and drowsiness that aids sleep. A man who is abstinent for a long period will have fewer of these episodic hormonal releases, which could mean missing out on their stress-relieving and sleep-promoting effects. That said, these effects can be obtained through other means (e.g. cuddling can also release oxytocin), and not all men report negative feelings from abstinence.

Women: Research on women’s hormonal responses to prolonged abstinence is more limited. Sexual activity in women is strongly tied to endogenous hormones like estrogen and progesterone that drive libido and reproductive cycles. Rather than abstinence altering hormone baselines, it may be that women with higher estradiol are more likely to be sexually active. One observational study found that sexually active women showed different hormone patterns across the menstrual cycle compared to abstinent women. In that study, women who engaged in regular partnered sex had significantly higher luteal-phase progesterone and a shift toward anti-inflammatory (“Th2”) immune cytokines, an adaptation thought to support potential pregnancy. By contrast, abstinent women did not show this progesterone rise or immune shift in the luteal phase. Notably, the same study found no significant difference in overall estradiol levels between sexually active and abstinent women. These findings suggest that regular sexual activity can modulate female hormonal and immune dynamics, whereas prolonged abstinence keeps the body in a more “baseline” state.
Like men, women also release oxytocin during sexual activity (particularly orgasm), which strengthens pair bonding and induces positive emotions. Without sexual intimacy, women may have fewer spikes in oxytocin, potentially impacting emotional bonding (discussed later). In summary, prolonged abstinence in both genders removes the acute hormone fluctuations from sexual activity; men might see a short-term uptick in testosterone, and women may experience a different hormonal profile, but no severe long-term hormonal imbalances from abstinence have been documented in healthy individuals.
Immune System and Infection Resistance
Sexual activity and abstinence can influence certain aspects of the immune system. Moderate sexual activity has been linked to enhanced immune function in some studies. For example, a classic finding (Charnetski & Brennan, 2004) was that people who have sex about once or twice a week had ~30% higher levels of salivary immunoglobulin A (IgA), an antibody marker of immune defense, compared to those with sex rarely or never. More recent research continues to explore this connection. A 2020 study noted that individuals with low sexual frequency had elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) levels – CRP is a marker of systemic inflammation . In that national sample, those who seldom had sex showed higher inflammation (along with other differences like being more often nonsmokers and more educated), suggesting a possible link between sexual inactivity and inflammatory immune status . On the other hand, having regular sex is associated with better immune resilience, such as one report finding more frequent sexual activity correlated with stronger immunity against certain infections. It’s important to recognize cause and effect are unclear – it may be that health issues lead to lower sexual activity rather than abstinence causing inflammation. Nonetheless, immune markers often differ between the sexually active and abstinent.
Interestingly, gender differences have emerged in immunity studies. Lorenz et al. (2014) examined interactions of sexual activity, depression, and immunity, and found that for women with depressive symptoms, being sexually active was linked to lowered immune function (lower IgA levels) compared to abstinent women, whereas for men, sexual activity tended to be a resilience factor (potentially boosting immune parameters). In other words, a depressed woman not having sex showed stronger IgA-mediated immunity than one who was sexually active, whereas in men the opposite trend was observed. This suggests that psychosocial context matters: involuntary or stressful sexual activity might tax women’s immune system, while men’s immunity might benefit from partnered sex even when depressed. In healthy women without depression, sexual activity appears to generally enhance certain immune responses; for example, sexually active women were found to have higher levels of several immune cytokines (both pro- and anti-inflammatory) than abstinent women, across the menstrual cycle. From an infection standpoint, abstinence of course eliminates the risk of sexually transmitted infections (STIs) entirely – a clear immune health benefit for the abstinent individual. Some people choose celibacy specifically to avoid STIs or because of immune concerns. On balance, current evidence indicates that regular sexual activity may provide immune benefits (moderating inflammation and enhancing certain antibody levels), but these effects can vary by gender and individual context. Long-term abstinence might modestly compromise some of these immune benefits in some people, though it also shields from sexually transmitted disease. Notably, no severe immune deficiencies are caused by abstaining; many abstinent individuals maintain normal immune health. The differences observed are often subtle or associated with other factors (like overall health or relationship status).
Sleep Quality and Fatigue
Sexual activity – especially orgasm – can influence sleep and relaxation. During sexual climax, the body releases hormones such as prolactin, which is associated with relaxation and drowsiness. This is one reason people often report improved sleep after satisfying sexual activity. Indeed, frequent sex has been linked to enhanced sleep quality due to these hormonal effects. Conversely, prolonged abstinence means an individual may not be leveraging this natural sleep aid. A person who has no sexual release for long periods might find other ways to relax, but if someone is used to sex as a sleep remedy, they might subjectively notice more difficulty sleeping when abstinent.
That said, research has not identified major sleep disorders resulting from abstinence itself. Any impact on fatigue or energy levels from not having sex appears to be minor or anecdotal. For instance, proponents of short-term abstinence (like the “No Nut November” trend, where participants avoid ejaculation for 30 days) claim it gives them more energy and less fatigue, but scientific evaluation finds no significant differences. A 2025 study specifically investigated “No Nut November” participants versus non-participants and found no measurable change in fatigue or energy levels, and no overall differences in sexual or psychological well-being after one month of abstinence. This controlled study debunked the internet anecdotes, showing that a month of abstinence had no clear benefits or harms on well-being (aside from participants reporting a bit more “sexual flexibility” in attitude).
Historically, there was a belief (especially in sports) that abstaining from sex conserves energy and boosts aggression, thus improving athletic performance. Modern research does not support a significant impact of pre-competition sexual abstinence on performance. A systematic review in 2025 found no conclusive effect of having sex versus abstaining on strength, endurance, or hormonal levels in athletes, although some abstinent athletes subjectively felt more focused. In terms of daily fatigue, regular sex can be moderate exercise that boosts circulation and triggers restful sleep, so its absence might slightly reduce those relaxing effects – but people compensate with other routines. Overall, sleep quality might improve with sexual activity for some (due to hormonal relaxation), but prolonged abstinence in itself does not cause insomnia or chronic fatigue according to current evidence. Any differences in energy are subtle and more related to stress or mood (which abstinence can affect indirectly, as discussed next).
Other Physical Health Considerations (Cardiovascular, Cancer, Prostate Health)
Long-term abstinence can intersect with other aspects of physical health. Regular sex is sometimes described as beneficial exercise for the heart. It increases heart rate and blood circulation, akin to light aerobic activity. Epidemiological research suggests a link between sexual frequency and longevity: lower sexual activity has been associated with higher mortality risk, particularly in women. A 2024 analysis of U.S. health survey data found that women who rarely had sex (less than once per week) were 70% more likely to die over about a decade than women who had sex at least weekly. Moreover, this study showed an interaction with mental health: among individuals with depression, those who had sex less than once a week had nearly 3 times higher mortality than depressed individuals who had sex around once per week . The authors concluded that sexual activity (around weekly or more) may be important for overall health and longevity, possibly through cardiovascular benefits and stress reduction . They caution, however, that poor health can also lead to reduced sexual activity, so causation is not certain . Notably, in that study the mortality association was strongest in women; in men the trend existed but was less pronounced (this gender difference is discussed later).
Sexual intimacy is linked to cardiovascular health via stress relief and hormonal effects. Orgasms trigger the release of oxytocin and endorphins that reduce stress hormones, which can lower blood pressure and promote a healthier heart rate variability . Over time, a satisfying sex life might contribute to better cardiovascular outcomes (as part of an overall healthy lifestyle). Abstinence, in contrast, might correlate with higher stress and blood pressure in some individuals. Indeed, regular sex is associated with lower blood pressure and reduced risk of cardiovascular events. If someone is involuntarily abstinent and stressed about it, that stress could negatively affect cardiovascular health. However, if one is comfortably abstinent, the lack of sexual activity alone is not known to cause heart problems.
In men, one specific health aspect is prostate health. Research indicates that frequent ejaculation may protect against prostate cancer. A 2018 meta-analysis (covering multiple studies) found that men who ejaculated more often (e.g. 2–4 times per week) had a significantly lower risk of prostate cancer compared to those who ejaculated less frequently. The mechanism might be that regular ejaculation helps clear the prostate of carcinogenic substances or reduces inflammation. Therefore, long-term abstinence (involving infrequent or no ejaculation) could potentially be associated with a higher risk of prostate issues over many years. Doctors sometimes encourage men to ejaculate periodically for prostate health, whether via sex or masturbation. It’s worth noting this is a statistical correlation; not every abstinent man will develop prostate problems, but population-wise, very low ejaculation frequency has been linked to slightly higher prostate cancer rates.
For women, an analogous physical consideration is pelvic floor health. Regular sexual activity (including intercourse or orgasm through masturbation) engages the pelvic floor muscles. Particularly, women who have sex frequently tend to maintain stronger pelvic floor tone, which supports bladder function. Sexual arousal and orgasm cause involuntary contractions of these muscles, effectively giving them a “workout.” This can help reduce incontinence and improve bladder control in women . A woman who abstains long-term might lose some of this conditioning of pelvic muscles, potentially increasing risk of urinary leakage with age. Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels) can compensate for this, so abstinent women aren’t destined to have problems, but the sexual exercise component is a noted benefit of an active sex life.

In summary, prolonged abstinence can be associated with certain physical health differences, including higher inflammation markers, possibly higher risks of some conditions (like prostate issues in men), and loss of some cardiovascular or muscular benefits that regular sex provides. However, it’s crucial to note that abstinence itself is often not the direct cause of poor health – rather, it might be one factor in a complex lifestyle and health profile. Someone who is otherwise active and manages stress well can be perfectly healthy while abstinent. The absence of sexual activity is not inherently detrimental to physical health for most people, but the associations found in large studies suggest that an active sex life often goes hand-in-hand with certain positive health indicators.
Mental Health and Psychological Effects of Abstinence
Prolonged sexual abstinence can impact mental health and emotional well-being, though the effects vary widely among individuals. Sexual intimacy can fulfill psychological needs for closeness, stress relief, and pleasure. Conversely, involuntary abstinence – especially when one desires sex but cannot have it – may lead to frustration or mental distress. Here we examine how abstinence relates to stress, anxiety, depression, mood, and relationship dynamics, again noting differences between men and women where applicable.
Stress and Anxiety
Regular sex is a known stress reducer for many. The biochemical cocktail released during sexual arousal and orgasm (oxytocin, endorphins, dopamine) produces feelings of relaxation and bonding, helping to lower stress levels. Studies during the COVID-19 lockdown in 2020 found that individuals or couples who maintained regular sexual activity had lower stress and anxiety related to sexual function compared to those with reduced sexual activity. Sex can also improve sleep (as discussed), which indirectly lowers stress. Therefore, abstaining from sex long-term might deprive individuals of a natural stress-relief avenue, potentially leaving baseline stress or anxiety a bit higher – particularly if the person is prone to worry or does not have alternative outlets.
When abstinence is voluntary or aligned with one’s preferences (for example, among asexual individuals or those practicing celibacy for personal reasons), it does not necessarily increase stress or anxiety. Such individuals often find peace with their choice and may even experience relief by avoiding sexual situations that would cause anxiety. On the other hand, involuntary abstinence (wanting sex but not having access to a partner or struggling with sexual dysfunction) can become a source of stress. Research on “incels” (involuntarily celibate individuals) and others who feel deprived of sex shows higher levels of frustration, loneliness, and anxiety in that population. The large 2025 study of sexless individuals (never had sex) found they were significantly more nervous and anxious on average than those who were sexually active. This suggests that a lack of intimate experience, especially when undesired, correlates with greater anxiety and neuroticism.
There is also evidence that extreme abstinence practices can exacerbate anxiety. The “NoFap” community, which advocates complete long-term abstinence from masturbation and porn, has reported
negative mental health effects. According to one analysis, individuals attempting lifelong sexual abstinence in these groups often experienced increased anxiety and even new onset of sexual dysfunction (e.g. erectile issues) as a result of the pressure and stress. In contrast, the short-term “No Nut November” challenge did not show a significant anxiety increase on average, likely because of its temporary nature and social support aspect. But overall, abstinence can remove a pleasurable stress outlet and, if not managed, may lead to heightened anxiety or tension in some people. Men who link their self-esteem to sexual success might be especially prone to anxiety when abstinent, whereas women might experience anxiety in abstinence more related to relationship concerns (e.g., “Does my partner still desire me?” if within a relationship).
Depression and Mood
Sexual intimacy and satisfaction are intertwined with mood and happiness. Regular consensual sex is associated with positive mood partly due to biochemical effects (oxytocin, endorphins, and the general pleasure of orgasm) and partly due to emotional fulfillment. Research consistently finds a correlation between infrequent sex and lower psychological well-being. For instance, one study found that people who have sex less than once a week are less happy on average than those having sex about once a week, even when controlling for relationship status. In fact, increasing sexual frequency up to one intercourse per week was linked to greater happiness, but additional frequency beyond that yielded no extra benefit. Thus, there appears to be a threshold of sexual connection (around weekly) that is associated with optimal well-being for many individuals. Those below that threshold – especially in a relationship context – often report lower life satisfaction.
Prolonged abstinence, particularly if not by choice, can contribute to feelings of sadness or depression. The large-scale 2025 PNAS study of adults who had never had sex revealed these sexless individuals were significantly lonelier and unhappier on average than their sexually experienced peers. It’s a striking finding that never having intimate physical connection correlates with higher loneliness and lower happiness scores. This doesn’t prove that abstinence causes unhappiness – it’s likely that some underlying factors (such as social anxiety or certain life circumstances) lead to both sexlessness and unhappiness. However, qualitative reports from long-abstinent people do often mention a sense of missing out, physical touch hunger, or emotional isolation that can feed into depression.
Gender plays a role in these psychological outcomes. The PNAS study noted that while sexlessness predicted loneliness and nervousness in both men and women, some effects were more pronounced in men. Sexless men’s well-being was tied more strongly to certain physical and social factors (like having fewer potential partners around, or having traits that made finding partners harder). Women in that study also experienced loneliness with sexlessness, but their unhappiness was slightly less tied to physical attributes and more to social/relationship context. In another aspect, an older longitudinal study on aging adults found that declines in sexual activity were associated with subsequent depressive symptoms, especially if the decline was not voluntary. Men who develop erectile dysfunction or other sexual problems sometimes become depressed as a result of the loss of sexual intimacy. In women, a drop in sexual desire or activity in midlife can coincide with mood declines, though this can be confounded by menopausal changes.
Conversely, improving one’s sex life can boost mood in those who desire more sex. Therapies for low sexual desire or performance (in both men and women) often report not only improved sexual frequency
but also significant improvements in depression or mood scales once a satisfying sexual connection is restored. This underscores the role sexual fulfillment can play in psychological health.
It’s important to mention that not everyone is negatively affected by abstinence. For some individuals, abstinence can coincide with positive mental health. If someone has a low intrinsic desire for sex or prioritizes other aspects of life, they may feel content or even relieved when not pursuing sexual relationships. Additionally, those practicing abstinence for religious or personal growth reasons might experience a sense of control or clarity that they find mentally beneficial. Thus, the mental health impact of sexual abstinence is highly individual. Broadly, though, population studies suggest that involuntary sexual inactivity is linked to lower mood and higher risk of depression, whereas voluntary abstinence does not carry the same negative effect and can be neutral or positive for mood.
Emotional Bonding and Relationship Dynamics
One of the most significant areas affected by prolonged abstinence is interpersonal relationships and emotional bonding. Sexual intimacy is a key component of romantic relationships for many couples, fostering closeness, trust, and affectionate bonding through shared vulnerability and the release of oxytocin (“the love hormone”). When couples stop having sex for long periods, it can alter the dynamics of the relationship. Some couples report maintaining emotional intimacy through other means, but others experience a decline in relationship satisfaction and an increase in conflicts or insecurities.
Research shows that couples who have sex about once a week or more tend to report higher relationship satisfaction and emotional well-being than those who have sex less frequently. Amy Muise and colleagues (2016) found that weekly sex was a kind of “sweet spot” for maximizing relationship happiness; having sex more often than that didn’t further increase happiness, but having it less often was associated with lower relationship quality. In line with this, a national longitudinal study of older adults highlighted the importance of regular partnered sex for both mental health and relationship quality as people age. When sex was less frequent than desired, partners often rated their relationship lower and experienced more depressive symptoms. Feeling sexually disconnected can lead to feelings of rejection or concern about the partner’s affection.
In sexless marriages or partnerships (commonly defined as having sex fewer than 10 times per year), studies estimate about 15–20% of long-term couples fall into this category. Many such couples remain emotionally close and some are content with low sexual frequency, especially if both partners have lower libido. In fact, roughly half of people in sexless marriages still report being happy in the relationship. These often involve partnerships where companionship, friendship, and other forms of intimacy (like cuddling, intellectual connection, etc.) take precedence over sex. However, for the other half, a lack of sex can be distressing. A sexless marriage can lead to decreased self-esteem, feelings of rejection or inadequacy, and increased anxiety or depression for one or both partners. Men, in particular, sometimes report feeling unloved or “emasculated” if their spouse shows no sexual interest, whereas women in sexless relationships might feel unwanted or worry that the partner’s lack of desire signals waning love. These feelings can erode emotional intimacy if not addressed.
Open communication is key in mitigating the negative relationship effects of abstinence. Couples who talk about their sexual needs and find alternative ways to be intimate often fare better. For instance, even if intercourse is off the table (due to health issues, distance, or choice), practicing other forms of intimacy – kissing, cuddling, affectionate touch – can maintain a couple’s bond. Many couples in long-term
abstinence situations (e.g., due to illness) adapt by emphasizing emotional support and non-sexual affection. As Medical News Today notes, romantic relationships can remain fulfilling without frequent sex, so long as other aspects of intimacy and communication are strong. That said, if there’s a mismatch in desires (one partner wants sex and the other is abstinent or disinterested), relationship strain often occurs. This can manifest as frustration, infidelity, or breakup if unresolved. Indeed, sexless marriages have a higher likelihood of divorce compared to sexually active marriages, especially when the lack of sex is accompanied by poor communication.
From a bonding hormone perspective, less sex means fewer spikes of oxytocin shared between partners during intimate moments. Oxytocin released during cuddling and orgasm reinforces pair bonds. Thus, prolonged abstinence might require couples to compensate with other bonding activities to achieve the same level of closeness. If successful, their emotional bond can remain strong. If not, partners may slowly drift emotionally. It’s notable that in the PNAS 2025 study, “sexless” individuals (never married or long-term singles with no sexual history) were on average lonelier, but this effect was less if they had strong social networks or platonic close relationships. This implies humans can fulfill some bonding needs through non-sexual relationships too. However, the unique bond of a sexual/romantic relationship has qualities (passion, physical affection) that are hard to replace completely.
In summary, prolonged sexual abstinence can challenge emotional bonding and relationship dynamics, but its impact depends on the context. When abstinence is mutually chosen or necessary, couples can maintain love and satisfaction through communication and non-sexual intimacy. Problems arise mostly when abstinence is involuntary or mismatched in a relationship – in those cases, it often correlates with lower relationship satisfaction and can negatively affect mental health for one or both partners. The key is that sex can be an important “glue” for many couples, and removing it requires conscious efforts to keep the bond strong in other ways.
Gender Differences in the Effects of Sexual Abstinence
While men and women share many common responses to prolonged abstinence (e.g. both may experience frustration or unchanged health depending on the situation), research points to some notable gender differences in both physiological and psychological outcomes:
Hormonal and Physiological Responses: Men experience more straightforward physiological changes with abstinence, such as transient increases in testosterone after short abstinence and potential prostate health implications from long-term low ejaculation frequency. Women’s physiology responds to sexual activity in cyclical ways – for example, sexually active women have higher luteal progesterone and immune shifts supportive of fertility, which abstinent women lack. However, women do not show a clear-cut “spike” in a hormone analogous to men’s testosterone surge; their estradiol levels are not significantly altered by abstinence. In essence, men’s bodies might react to a lack of ejaculation with a buildup of semen and slight hormone elevation, whereas women’s bodies when abstinent simply forego the subtle hormonal enhancements that accompany regular sexual activity.
Immune Function: There is evidence of sex-specific immune responses related to sexual activity. As noted, one study found that depressed women who were sexually active had lower IgA immune levels than depressed women who were abstinent, whereas in men sexual activity tended to correlate with stronger immunity. Thus, the impact of abstinence on immunity might differ by gender under certain conditions (e.g., depression), with women potentially more susceptible to immune changes due to sexual activity or lack thereof.
Mental Health – Mood and Loneliness: Both men and women can experience negative mood effects from involuntary abstinence, but men may be more likely to externalize frustration as irritability or depression, whereas women might internalize it as loneliness or self-blame. The 2025 “life without sex” study reported that sexless men showed a stronger association with certain physical and social traits – for instance, many sexless men had lower upper-body strength and lived in environments with fewer potential female partners. This suggests some men’s lack of sexual activity is tied to external factors (like physical health or demographics) which can also affect their self-esteem and mood. Women’s sexlessness, on the other hand, was less about physical strength and more uniformly distributed, implying social or personal choice factors might play a bigger role for women. Nonetheless, loneliness and unhappiness due to lack of intimacy were observed in both genders. One difference noted in relational contexts is that men in sexless marriages often report feeling a lack of validation or masculinity, which can lead to anger or deep sadness (“Why doesn’t she desire me?”), whereas women more commonly report feeling unattractive or anxious (“Is he no longer in love with me?”). These are general trends; either gender can feel any of those emotions.
Stress and Coping: Men and women might cope differently with abstinence. Some studies indicate that men’s stress levels may be more immediately reduced by sexual release (hence the traditional idea of masturbation or sex as a stress outlet for men), and removing that outlet can make some men more tense or prone to distraction. Women, whose sexual desire is often more context-dependent, might not rely on sex as heavily for day-to-day stress relief; they may substitute emotional closeness or social support. However, women may experience heightened stress if abstinence makes them worry about relationship stability, as security in a relationship is often tied to intimacy for many women. Men, particularly younger men, sometimes channel sexual frustration into aggressiveness or risk-taking if not managed, whereas women might experience more anxiety or depressive rumination in similar scenarios. It’s important to emphasize individual variation, but gender socialization plays a role in these stress responses.
Relationship Impact: If a heterosexual couple is dealing with prolonged abstinence, men and women can perceive it differently. Studies have found that a mismatch in desired sexual frequency strongly predicts poorer relationship quality and mental health in both genders, but sometimes one gender is less satisfied than the other. For example, a man might be distressed by a sexless relationship while his female partner is content with emotional closeness only, or vice versa. Culturally, men are often expected to be always ready for sex, so an abstinent man (especially if it’s due to low desire or dysfunction) may feel significant shame or reduced self-worth. Women might face societal judgment as well (e.g., stereotypes about “old maids” or assumptions something is wrong if they’re not sexually active), but generally men report greater stigma around not having sexual experience, which can affect their social confidence. Moderate frequency (2–4×/week) was also protective relative to very infrequent ejaculation. This suggests prolonged periods of abstinence (very low ejaculation) may slightly increase long-term prostate cancer risk, possibly due to buildup of prostatic fluids or inflammatory factors. Health recommendation: regular sexual release might be beneficial for prostate health.

Pre-Competition Abstinence in Athletes (2025)

Marital Sex and Relationship Quality (2015)
Kaplan et al. – Journals of Gerontology: Psychological Sciences
732 married couples in midlife and older (US), surveyed over 10 years.
Measured frequency of marital sex (from “never” to “weekly+”) and tracked relationship satisfaction and depressive symptoms.
Couples reporting no sex or very infrequent sex (<10 times/ year) had, on average, lower relationship satisfaction and higher depressive symptoms than those with more frequent sex. The effect was especially pronounced if the desired frequency mismatched the actual – i.e., if one or both spouses wanted more sex than occurred, both relationship quality and individual mental health suffered. Over time, couples with persistent sexual inactivity were more likely to have considered divorce or to report emotional distance. However, a subset of low-frequency couples who were mutually content (no partner desired more sex) did not exhibit poorer relationship or mental health – their satisfaction remained high despite abstinence. This suggests that it is often unmet sexual needs, rather than abstinence per se, that harms relationships. Gender note: the association between sex frequency and happiness was similar for husbands and wives, underscoring that both men and women in the study benefited from a sexual connection for marital well-being.

Conclusion
In the past decade of research, prolonged sexual abstinence has been associated with a mix of neutral, positive, and negative effects on health – many of which depend on whether abstinence is by choice and on the individual’s life context. Physically, a lack of sexual activity is not inherently harmful for most; abstinent individuals do not suffer gross hormonal deficiencies or immune collapse. Men and women abstaining from sex generally maintain normal baseline health. However, they may miss out on certain health benefits that regular sex can confer, such as improved immune markers, better sleep, and (for men) potentially lower risk of prostate issues. Abstinence can correlate with higher physiological stress and, in older men, might signal underlying health concerns if it coincides with loss of function. Mentally and emotionally, the impact of abstinence is highly variable. For many, especially those who desire sexual connection, long periods without sex can elevate stress, anxiety, and feelings of loneliness or depression. Large-scale studies illustrate that involuntary sexlessness often goes hand-in-hand with reduced happiness and social connection. At the same time, people who purposefully choose abstinence (for personal, religious, or asexual identity reasons) can maintain robust mental health, indicating that the personal meaning of abstinence is crucial.
When comparing genders, men and women show some different patterns in response to abstinence. Men might experience more immediate physiological changes (like fluctuating testosterone) and may tie sexual activity more to self-esteem, sometimes responding to abstinence with frustration or health-seeking behavior (or unfortunately, via negative outlets). Women may not have the same hormonal swings from abstinence but can be equally affected by the loss of intimacy, often internalizing it as emotional distance or insecurity in relationships. Notably, health correlations (like sexual activity and longevity) have appeared at least as strong in women as in men, if not stronger, hinting that regular intimacy may be an important component of wellness, especially for women in midlife and beyond. Both sexes benefit from affectionate contact and both can suffer psychologically from isolation – it’s a human need, not just a male or female need.
In summary, prolonged sexual abstinence has a multifaceted impact: - Hormones: Minor shifts (e.g. transient higher testosterone in men), but no drastic long-term hormonal harm; loss of oxytocin surges that come with orgasm. - Immune Function: Regular sex is linked to certain immune benefits (lower inflammation, higher IgA) , while abstinence might slightly elevate some risk markers; effects can differ by gender and context (e.g., depressed women might actually do better immune-wise abstinent). - Sleep and Fatigue: Sex can improve sleep; abstinence removes that aid but otherwise has no major effect on fatigue—recent studies show no boost in energy from short-term abstinence. - Stress and Mood: Sex is a stress reliever and mood booster for many; extended abstinence can increase stress and worsen mood if undesired. Involuntary abstinence is associated with higher anxiety and depression levels on average. -Relationship Dynamics: Sex is one of several pillars of intimacy. Long-term lack of sex in couples can erode satisfaction and closeness unless both partners are truly content with abstinence. Good communication and alternative intimacies can buffer this, but mismatched desires often lead to relationship strain.
Crucially, context matters. A healthy lifestyle, emotional support, and personal attitudes can mediate the effects of abstinence. As one review article concluded, “There is no correct amount of sex to have, and the best frequency varies from person to person. Not having sex for a long time should not have negative health effects” for most people . Avoiding sex will not inherently harm health and can even be positive for those who prefer it . However, for individuals who value sexual connection, prolonged abstinence can be a significant psycho-social stressor that subtly affects their well-being and certain health parameters.

Ultimately, the decision or circumstance of sexual abstinence is a personal one. Modern research suggests that maintaining overall health is possible with or without sex – but the presence of a satisfying sex life often accompanies better mental health, stronger relationships, and some physical health benefits. Men and women alike should be mindful of how sexual inactivity affects them personally. If someone experiences negative symptoms (e.g. depressive feelings, relationship issues) during abstinence, they might consider speaking with a healthcare professional or therapist. Conversely, those who are abstinent and happy can be reassured that they are not “damaging” their health. Prolonged abstinence is compatible with health and happiness, but its impacts are nuanced, tending to mirror an individual’s broader emotional and social fulfillment.
