Letter to Sapphire V2
Sapphire,

You told me I wasn’t your soulmate. That if I had really loved you, I never would have betrayed you. You said I knew all you’d gone through (what your mother did, what your father did, all the trauma) and still, I hurt you. And maybe that’s true. Maybe I did hurt you. But you need to sit with the truth that you hurt me too… deeply, consistently, and in ways I don’t even think you’re ready to own yet.

You told me that if I truly loved you, I would have stayed. That I would have let my needs rot quietly inside me while I continued to pour into you. Even though you read the report about my father’s abuse. Even though you saw how sexual frustration broke him (how decades of being denied, diminished, and emotionally castrated hollowed him out from the inside) and you still asked me to stay. You still wanted me to accept that same path, walk toward that same slow death, and pretend that love made that noble. You wanted me to repress the exact same hunger that destroyed him. You read that with your own eyes. And still, you said, “Stay with me.”

I couldn’t even finish those reports. My trauma wouldn’t let me. But you did. You read all of it. And you still wanted to confine me. You still wanted to silence my body, my needs, my entire sexual being… indefinitely. You were okay letting me rot. You were okay watching me scream in silence.

It would be one thing if you simply didn’t understand. But you did understand. You just didn’t care enough to sacrifice your frameworks of control. You wanted me to live inside a monogamous structure that you knew was breaking me… just so you could feel safe. Just so you wouldn’t have to confront your own scarcity mindset. Just so you wouldn’t have to reckon with the reality that your ability to satisfy me sexually had limits.

Let’s stop pretending this was about love.

If you truly loved me, you would not have demanded I abandon myself to preserve you. If you truly loved me, you wouldn’t have weaponized monogamy (a system you know is a tool of white supremacy, patriarchy, and colonial violence) to cage me. You say you’re about unlearning white supremacy, but you clung to a relationship model that was designed to oppress. You refused to do the decolonizing work when it came to your own intimacy. You were perfectly willing to keep upholding those systems when they worked in your favor.

Because I made you feel safe. Because I poured into you. Because my body was present, even when my needs weren’t met. You wanted the benefits of my presence… without having to show up fully for me.

And you called it betrayal when I couldn’t keep living like that.

You say I didn’t love you, but I did. I do. The kind of love that sees someone in their totality. That sees their beauty, their rage, their trauma. That holds space. That tries to fight for them. But I wasn’t willing to die for it. I wasn’t willing to amputate a part of myself just so you could feel whole.

You always struggled with control. You admitted that. You wanted to feel in control of everything, especially of me. You tried to dress it up as love, but it was possessiveness. It was fear. And when I tried to talk to you about my sexual needs (my pain, my hunger, the mismatch) you made it about your trauma again. You said I should’ve done more work. That I should’ve healed more. That I should’ve endured more. You expected me to keep showing up, to keep giving, while quietly accepting that my needs would never be met.

You said monogamy was a line you couldn’t cross. You said you weren’t enough for me, and instead of confronting that, you turned it into a punishment. I could either deny my sexuality or lose you entirely. That was the binary you set up. And you called it love.

And here’s the truth, Sapphire… you weren’t enough. Not sexually. Not intimately. Not in the way I needed. And that’s not even an indictment. That’s just the truth. Because I wanted you to be. I tried to make you enough. I contorted myself. I shrank my needs. I held back tears during countless nights of rejection and silence and cold distance. I fought for you harder than I’ve ever fought for anything. But the moment I stopped sacrificing myself at the altar of your comfort, you said I never loved you.

What hurts most is that I thought our love transcended this. I thought it was revolutionary. I thought it could hold complexity. I thought it could survive honesty. But it couldn’t even survive a conversation about unmet needs. It couldn’t survive my desire for ethical non-monogamy… even though you knew exactly what that meant for me. Even though you saw the work I’ve done. The reading. The reflecting. The intellectualizing. The effort to create frameworks rooted in autonomy, mutual care, and transparency.

You didn’t want to build anything with me. You just wanted me to keep driving the boat you picked out of a white supremacist catalog. And if I wanted to build something of our own (something intentional, something liberatory) you called it betrayal.

You didn’t want partnership. You wanted ownership.

So now I sit here wondering if you ever loved me at all. Or if you just loved the way I made you feel. If you just loved the safety, the certainty, the way I poured into you even when I was starving. Maybe that’s what you thought love was. Maybe that’s all you had the capacity for.

But I know what love is now. It doesn’t cage. It doesn’t silence. It doesn’t withhold. Love demands growth. Love holds complexity. Love expands. And what we had… it was love, maybe, but only up to the point where it asked you to change.

I’m still doing the work. Every single day. I’m excavating years of generational trauma, learning how to hold space for others without losing myself in the process. I’m trying to become the kind of partner I never saw growing up. I’m still learning how to give and receive love without making it a transaction or a sacrifice of autonomy.

And I will always love you. Not the version you are right now… but the version I believed you could be. The version I still hope you might become.

But I couldn’t wait anymore. Not if waiting meant withering.

—Emmanuel
