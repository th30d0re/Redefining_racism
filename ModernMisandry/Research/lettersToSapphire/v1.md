Letter to sapphire V1

To Sapphire

I’ve spent a lot of time reflecting—on what went wrong, on how we got here, and on what I failed to do when it mattered most. I want to say this without ego or deflection. I’m not trying to rewrite the past or rescue my image. I’m writing this so that you hear the full truth, from me—not filtered through anger, assumptions, or silence.

Where I Failed

I emotionally checked out before I had the courage to say it out loud. I should’ve been honest the moment I realized we had reached an impasse, but I was scared. Scared of breaking your heart. Scared of breaking my own. I held on longer than I should have, and instead of ending things cleanly, I detached in silence. That wasn’t fair to you. And I own that.

I waited until after our last real chance had passed. I told myself I was delaying the breakup to spare you the pain before Valentine’s Day, but the truth is… I was trying to soften a blow that was always going to land hard. And instead of honesty, I gave you false hope.

The moment I downloaded dating apps before we were officially over… I crossed a line. Even though nothing physical had happened yet, it was still a betrayal. You found out from friends, not me. And for that—I am genuinely sorry.

But what hurts most is that all of this—the cheating, the detachment, the silence—has become the sole story of our breakup. When the truth is far more complicated.

What You Never Saw

I lived in restraint for years. I cuddled naked next to a woman I loved deeply but could not touch for months at a time. I stayed through medical challenges and sexual deprivation that left me angry, numb, and eventually… invisible.

You say I lacked sexual discipline—but I was starving, and I stayed.

You say I used you—but I housed you, protected you, supported you when no one else did.

You say I never tried—but I bent over backward trying to force a version of myself that could live without feeling desired. That could live without intimacy. That could live with being unseen.

And when I couldn’t keep it up anymore, I snapped. And instead of seeing that collapse as the end of a long unraveling, you chose to treat it like the first thread that tore us apart.

It wasn’t. The truth is: we were already done.

What I’ve Learned

I spoke to both my parents after we ended. And what I found broke me.

My dad spoke with the same exact pain and frustration I felt. Years of monogamy without intimacy turned him bitter. Angry. Self-destructive. I saw myself in him and it terrified me.

My mom, meanwhile, spoke the same words you once did. She felt used, disconnected, unseen. For her, sex without emotional fulfillment felt hollow. So she withdrew.

That split—the one between masculine and feminine desire, between how we give and how we feel safe receiving—is generational. It tore my parents apart. And it tore us apart, too.

And it showed me that monogamy, in the way most people practice it, doesn’t work for me. Not because I don’t want to commit—but because commitment without autonomy, without desire flowing both ways, leads to rot.

That’s what happened to us. We both decayed silently, until the break became undeniable.

What I Still Struggle With

I never wanted to be the bad guy. And I never thought you’d paint me that way so completely.

Yes, I failed. But you could have left too. You saw it wasn’t working. You knew you couldn’t meet my needs. And instead of walking away, you wanted me to keep trying harder—even when you had no answers. Even when your “compromise” was still centered only around your experience of intimacy.

And when I finally cracked—after years of trying—you used that moment to define me.

You said I wouldn’t disclose my diagnosis. As if the man who stayed in a sexually constrained relationship for years—who got the virus because he stayed—would then go out into the world and lie about it.

You never saw how much I was holding back.
You never saw how hard I tried to love you, even while breaking inside.

And maybe you never will.

My Final Truth

This isn’t a plea for forgiveness. It’s not a justification. It’s just what’s true:

I loved you. Deeply. I believed you were my soulmate. And I still believe that, had we not gotten herpes, we might’ve stayed together.

But the truth is: we did. And it changed everything. It created fear, shame, distance… and in time, we both weaponized those things against each other in different ways.

I’m not proud of how I exited.
But I’m proud that I learned.

I won’t repeat the cycle. I will lead with clarity and autonomy in every connection I build moving forward. I will never again betray myself to try and make a relationship work that’s already dying.

I hope you find what you need. I hope you feel seen and wanted and poured into.

But most of all, I hope you find someone who understands your pain—because I really tried to. I just didn’t have the tools to hold mine and yours at the same time.

Thank you for loving me, even in the ways that hurt. You’ll always be part of my story.

With respect,
Emmanuel Theodore
