Letter to Sapphire V3  

Sapphire,

I’ve spent a lot of time reflecting… on what went wrong, on how we got here, and on what I failed to do when it mattered most. I want to say this without ego or deflection. I’m not trying to rewrite the past or rescue my image. I’m writing this so that you hear the full truth, from me… not filtered through anger, assumptions, or silence.

Chapter 0: Accountability; Where I Failed

I emotionally checked out before I had the courage to say it out loud. I should’ve been honest the moment I realized we had reached an impasse, but I was scared. Scared of breaking your heart. Scared of breaking my own. I held on longer than I should have, and instead of ending things cleanly, I detached in silence. That wasn’t fair to you. And I own that.

I waited until after our last real chance had passed. I told myself I was delaying the breakup to spare you the pain before Valentine’s Day, but the truth is… I was trying to soften a blow that was always going to land hard. And instead of honesty, I gave you false hope.

The moment I downloaded dating apps before we were officially over… I crossed a line. Even though nothing physical had happened yet, it was still a betrayal. You found out from friends, not me. And for that… I am genuinely sorry.

But what hurts most is that all of this (the cheating, the detachment, the silence) has become the sole story of our breakup. When the truth is far more complicated.

Chapter 1: The Myth of Soulmates and the Cost of Projection

You told me I wasn’t your soulmate. That if I had truly loved you, I never would have betrayed you. That the very fact of my departure (of my withdrawal) was proof that my love had been conditional, incomplete, performative. That someone who really cared would have endured it all without flinching. That I should have known better, because I knew your trauma. I knew your story. And still, I left.

But what if the betrayal wasn’t in leaving… but in staying too long?

What if staying would have meant betraying myself? What if staying would have reduced me to a hollow version of myself… a cautionary tale like my father, who gave and gave and gave until there was nothing left?

You knew that story. You read the words I couldn’t even finish. You looked into the abyss of that lineage and still asked me to keep walking toward it. That’s what breaks my heart. That you knew. That you saw. And still, you stayed silent. Still, you let me walk blind into the same fire that consumed generations of men before me.

---

**Chapter 2: The Illusion of Monogamy as Liberation**

You say you are a feminist. You say you want to unlearn white supremacy, capitalism, heteropatriarchy. And yet, when intimacy got hard, when sex got rare, when the infrastructure of our emotional ecosystem started to crumble—you didn’t lean into growth. You leaned into control.

You held tight to the very thing you claim to critique: monogamy as ownership. Monogamy as submission. Monogamy as an institutional script that privileges comfort over consciousness.

It’s wild how loudly you advocated for decolonization… until it touched your personal fears. Until it asked you to expand your definition of love. Until it asked you to let go of control.

And I get it. That fear is real. You were hurt. You were abandoned. You’ve had your agency stolen. But what you don’t seem to see is this: in the name of protecting yourself, you tried to colonize me. My sexuality. My autonomy. My peace.

---

**Chapter 3: Sexual Starvation and the Performance of Empathy**

For years, I lived inside a paradox. Cuddling every night with a naked woman I desired more than anything… and yet, unable to touch her. Watching the cadence of our sex life disintegrate into something chaotic, unpredictable, and increasingly avoidant. Refusing to shame you for your pain. Refusing to coerce. Refusing to withdraw love, even as I withered.

And yet… you turned around and said I lacked sexual discipline.

How? After all that restraint. All that silence. All those nights where I stayed soft when I was dying inside. Where I soothed your body while mine was screaming. Where I held back urges so deeply rooted in biology, in identity, in connection—because I didn’t want to become the man my father became.

You knew what that required of me. You saw it. And still… you told yourself I was the selfish one. That I was the addict. That I was the one who didn’t understand sacrifice.

---

**Chapter 4: Monogamy as a Cage You Kept Me In**

Let’s stop pretending we didn’t both know it was over. The only difference is that I tried to end it… and you asked me to stay. You didn’t offer solutions. You offered denial. You didn’t meet me in vulnerability. You met me with vague promises and unspoken expectations. You said you would “try”—but that try was only ever framed around your own needs. Your own comfort. Your own model of sex and intimacy.

Not once did you try to understand my model.

Not once did you say, “What does it feel like to be in your body, in this relationship, in this pain?”

You wanted a relationship where you didn’t have to change—but I had to keep giving. Where you didn’t have to meet me sexually—but I had to meet you emotionally. You held me hostage in a structure that no longer served us, and then blamed me for escaping.

That’s not love. That’s possession.

---

**Chapter 5: The Soulmate You Never Tried to See**

You say I wasn’t your soulmate. Maybe you’re right. Maybe I was just a vessel. A safe place. A temporary home. But the tragedy is… I thought you were mine.

I thought you were the one I could build something new with. I thought our love could transcend colonial blueprints. I thought you believed in reimagining relationship as a space for mutual healing—not mutual captivity.

But now I see. You didn’t want new blueprints. You wanted the comfort of familiar cages. Even when they were killing both of us.

---

**Chapter 6: Autonomy, Integrity, and the Future I Choose**

I will never again stay in a love that demands my silence.

I will never again trade my sexuality for a seat at the table of someone else’s fear.

I will never again shrink myself to preserve someone else’s fantasy of control.

You said I didn’t love you because I left.

I say: I loved you so much, I tried to stay. Until it cost me my soul.

I loved you enough to endure starvation. To endure shame. To endure disease. I held you through trauma. I housed you when you were homeless. I poured into you when I was empty.

And when I finally stepped away… you made me the villain.

But I am not ashamed.

I am awake.

I am healing.

I am building a future where love is not built on sacrifice—but on sovereignty. Where intimacy is not a currency—but a collaboration. Where partners do not own each other—but expand each other.

This isn’t revenge.

This is release.

This is my final offering: the truth.

With clarity and compassion,  
—Emmanuel
