Letter to Sapphire V4

I keep playing it back in my head…
Maybe if I were more vocal…
More emotional…
More vulnerable…
Maybe if I cried… if I broke down in front of you… Would you have taken me seriously then?
 
Because I tried to tell you. I tried to articulate it in all the ways I knew how. But every time I brought it up, you’d shut down. And so I would retreat, thinking I was only making it worse. I started convincing myself that maybe I was asking for too much… that maybe I should just hold it down in silence and keep pouring into you until something shifted. But the longer it went unaddressed, the more invisible I felt.
 
And the thing is… You didn’t believe me when I said it hurt.
Not just emotionally, but physically.
You didn’t believe men could feel pain from sexual frustration.
You didn’t believe that I’d walk around with a smile while my chest was caving in. That I’d go numb in the middle of conversations. That I was breaking down inside from a need that never stopped screaming. And all that time… I was still pouring into you, while begging quietly (respectfully) for you to pour into me.
 
Ask ChatGPT (or anyone grounded in reality) if most men would stay in a relationship where sex only happened every 2–3 weeks… or once a month… or once every two months… for years. Over 3 years.
 
It’ll tell you what I already knew from living it:
·       That men build emotional intimacy through physical intimacy.
·       That when sex becomes rare, we start to feel rejected, unseen, and unwanted.
·       That we don’t leave right away… but we do break.
·       And when there’s no empathy for that break, we become shells… still present, but not alive in the relationship anymore.
 
What makes it worse is… I didn’t want to detach from you. I didn’t want distance. I wanted closeness. I wanted relief. I wanted us.
You say you were frustrated too… and I believe you.
But the difference is… I was the one asking for help.
I was the one naming the issue. I was the one begging for solutions.
And I wasn’t trying to take anything from you. I wasn’t demanding anything harsh or cold. I was just asking… please don’t leave me in this state alone.
 
But instead of seeing my exhaustion as a warning… you saw it as weakness.
You treated my breaking point like an inconvenience.
And that’s when I knew:
You weren’t going to fight for me.
You weren’t going to see me.
You weren’t going to meet me with mutuality.
You were only going to rebut me.
To defend your position.
To protect your narrative.
 
And that… broke my heart in a way I’ve never fully recovered from.
 
For years, I lived inside a paradox. Cuddling every night with a naked woman I desired more than anything… and yet, unable to touch her. Watching the cadence of our sex life disintegrate into something chaotic, unpredictable, and increasingly avoidant. Refusing to shame you for your pain. Refusing to coerce. Refusing to withdraw love, even as I withered.
 
And yet… you turned around and said I lacked sexual discipline.
 
How? After all that restraint. All that silence. All those nights where I stayed soft when I was dying inside. Where I soothed your body while mine was screaming. Where I held back urges so deeply rooted in biology, in identity, in connection… because I didn’t want to become the man my father became.
 
You knew what that required of me. You saw it. And still… you told yourself I was the selfish one. That I was the addict. That I was the one who didn’t understand sacrifice.
 
 
Let’s stop pretending we didn’t both know it was over. The only difference is that I tried to end it… and you asked me to stay. You didn’t offer solutions. You offered denial. You didn’t meet me in vulnerability. You met me with vague promises and unspoken expectations. You said you would “try”… but that try was only ever framed around your own needs. Your own comfort. Your own model of sex and intimacy.
 
Not once did you try to understand my model.
 
Not once did you say, “What does it feel like to be in your body, in this relationship, in this pain?”
 
You wanted a relationship where you didn’t have to change… but I had to keep giving. Where you didn’t have to meet me sexually… but I had to meet you emotionally. You held me hostage in a structure that no longer served us, and then blamed me for escaping.
 
The second conversation…
That was when it really shifted for me.
I went into it already knowing it was probably over. I even said it: “We need to break up.”
But you begged me to stay. You offered solutions, yes—but none that actually addressed the issue I was crying out about.
You couldn’t—or wouldn’t—meet me in the place I was asking to be seen.
You knew what I needed… but still asked me to stay without giving me what I needed to survive.
And I… I should’ve stood on it.
I should’ve stayed firm in that truth and walked away.
But I couldn’t bring myself to say it again.
Even though I was already slipping away emotionally… even though part of me knew I was done…
I stayed.
Not out of hope… but out of disbelief.
I needed to prove to myself that I was really done.
And looking back… that was the moment I knew I was.
 
Because I didn’t check out of this relationship the moment I got frustrated.
I stayed. Longer than most men would.
Because I loved you.
Because I thought we were forever.
And maybe that’s why this still hurts the way it does
 
And I need to say this too… gently, respectfully but clearly:
 
That’s not love. That’s possession.
 
And here’s the truth, Sapphire… you weren’t enough. Not sexually. Not intimately. Not in the way I needed. And that’s not even an indictment. That’s just the truth. Because I wanted you to be. I tried to make you enough. I contorted myself. I shrank my needs. I held back tears during countless nights of rejection and silence and cold distance. I fought for you harder than I’ve ever fought for anything. But the moment I stopped sacrificing myself at the altar of your comfort, you said I never loved you.
 
I understand why women have every reason to be angry at men.
I understand the trauma. The history. The centuries of violence, control, and abuse.
But that pain… when unprocessed… it hardens.
And when it leaks into the space of a relationship, it stops being protection—it becomes projection.
It poisons intimacy. It turns your partner into your oppressor. And I felt that.
 
If your partner is a man… and you fundamentally don’t trust the male perspective… if you believe that his sexuality is suspect, his pain is irrelevant, and his desire is inherently selfish… then what are you building with him? How is that partnership?
 
Because men are not women.
Many of us are wired to feel closeness through touch, safety through sex, and love through being wanted.
That’s not immaturity. That’s not weakness. That’s neurobiology.
And if that truth is inconvenient, or feels incompatible with your values or lived experience—then maybe what you want and what a male partner offers just don’t align.
 
If what you truly need is someone who can build emotional closeness first and sustain sexual patience indefinitely… maybe it’s worth considering whether a woman might better understand that emotional cadence.
Because if you can’t believe in men… if you hate men—or what they represent… then it’s not fair to keep loving one halfway.
That’s not healing. That’s misalignment. And it hurts everyone involved.
 
I’m not saying all this to win an argument or rehash the past.
I’m saying it because I still care.
And I need you to know the full truth—finally, fully, and clearly.
 
And yes… I’m generalizing when I talk about how many men experience intimacy. Not all men are like this. Not all women are different. I know that. I’m naming patterns—not writing laws.
 
In fact, one of my closest friends—Isis—is going through the exact same thing right now.
But she’s a woman.
And I need you to really hear this… because she’s not just “sexually frustrated.” She’s unraveling.
 
She’s told me about the nights she snapped on her boyfriend—how she threw things, how she screamed, how she felt like she was going crazy. She’s broken down, mid-argument, mid-dinner, mid-silence, because the frustration of being unseen and untouched had built so high inside her that it started spilling out in chaos. She told me she’s gone to work carrying that same heaviness. That it ruined her mood. That she started having thoughts that scared her. That the pain of loving someone who can’t fulfill her basic needs made her feel like maybe she just wasn’t meant to be here at all.
 
And hearing her… watching her sob on FaceTime, I realized: That’s what I felt too.
I just never showed it.
I never threw things. I never yelled. I never spiraled in front of you.
But I felt all of it. I was drowning in it. I just internalized it.
And sometimes, I wonder… maybe that was the mistake.
Maybe if I’d screamed, if I’d cried, if I’d collapsed under the weight of it in front of you…
Maybe then you would’ve believed me. Maybe then you would’ve seen me.
 
Because what Isis is going through? It’s identical. She’s still in love with the man who can’t satisfy her… and she doesn’t want anyone else. She’s found someone who can give her the kind of sex that leaves her shaking… but it doesn’t matter, because she doesn’t love him. She wants her partner back. But she also wants her sanity. Her peace. Her body back.
 
And I feel the same.
I don’t want anyone else. I want you.
But it’s torturous when the person you see as your soulmate is no longer sexually compatible with you.
It breaks something deep inside.
And it hurts to know that even when I came to you—calmly, respectfully, lovingly—and said “I can’t anymore”… that you still couldn’t offer solutions that considered my reality.
That instead of picking up that baton, you asked me to keep running until I collapsed.
 
So maybe… maybe if hearing it from me doesn’t move you, maybe Isis could.
Because it’s not just me.
It’s not just men.
 
And if you’re open… we could set up a three-way FaceTime. Not to argue. Not to point fingers.
But just to talk.
To hear. To listen. To see the pain you couldn’t see in me… reflected in someone you might feel safer understanding.
Someone who’s been with violent men. Who’s been harmed. Who understands your trauma… but who also knows what it’s like to suffer like me—and not blame her partner. Not punish him for her past. Not project that pain onto someone trying to love her the best he can.
 
And if you want to feel safe, know this:
Isis and I haven’t had sex in years. She doesn’t have herpes. She knows I do.
There’s no threat here. No subtext.
This is just someone I trust. Someone who knows how to speak what I’ve been carrying.
 
You always believed in mutual aid. In community healing. In unlearning harmful dynamics.
Maybe… just maybe… this is a chance to do that.
To try a new conversation.
To speak the unspeakable.
And maybe, in doing so, find just a little more clarity for all of us.
And for full transparency—yes, I used ChatGPT to help me organize my thoughts. To check my tone. To make sure I wasn’t being cruel or reactive or saying things in a way that would invalidate your pain. But if you want the raw versions of everything I’ve written—I saved them. I’ll share them, if you want.
 
Because I’m not hiding.
I’ve just finally learned to speak.
And whether or not we ever find peace together, I at least need you to know what my silence was hiding all along.
 
—Emmanuel
