## 2. What the opinion actually holds

**Scalia majority (7-2; Scalia, joined by Rehnquist, O'Connor, Kennedy, Souter, Thomas, Breyer; Stevens and Ginsburg dissenting):**

The holding:

> "We hold that respondent did not, for purposes of the Due Process Clause, have a property interest in police enforcement of the restraining order against her husband." — 545 U.S. 748, 768 (2005).

On why mandatory-arrest language does not create an enforceable right:

> "Although Colorado's mandatory-arrest law spoke in the imperative, this Court's cases establish that a benefit is not a protected entitlement if government officials may exercise discretion in providing it." — 545 U.S. at 760–761.

**Stevens/Ginsburg dissent (gendered enforcement-duty gap):**

Stevens's dissent argued that a mandatory-arrest statute with no discretion language creates an enforceable entitlement. Ginsburg joined, noting the gendered dimension: the restraining order is the specific legal instrument designed for domestic violence victims — women disproportionately — and the majority's ruling eliminates the only legal tool designed to create an enforcement obligation in the highest-risk intimate-partner violence context.

\footnote{In \textit{Castle Rock}, Justice Scalia held for the majority: ``A benefit
is not a protected entitlement if government officials may exercise discretion in
providing it.'' 545 U.S.\ 748, 760--761 (2005) \cite{castlerock}. The structural
logic: Colorado's mandatory-arrest statute said the police ``shall'' arrest a
restrained person violating an order---but because the statute permitted any
discretion at all, no individual victim acquired an enforceable constitutional
entitlement to that arrest. The restraining order becomes a document without
obligation: the state creates it, issues it, and retains the discretion not to
honor it. Every DV victim who carries one carries a piece of paper that the
majority opinion holds to be constitutionally unenforceable as a protection mechanism.}
