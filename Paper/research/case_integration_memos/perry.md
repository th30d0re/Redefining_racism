## 2. What the opinion actually holds

**Hughes majority (5-4; Hughes, joined by Brandeis, Stone, Roberts, Cardozo; McReynolds, Van Devanter, Sutherland, Butler dissenting):**

The key structural feature of Perry: the majority held that voiding the gold-clause contract was technically unconstitutional ("We are of the opinion that the Joint Resolution of June 5, 1933, insofar as it attempted to override the obligation of the gold clause in the government bond is unconstitutional") — but simultaneously held that the plaintiff could not recover damages because receiving legal tender (even devalued) fulfilled the nominal dollar obligation. The constitutional violation was identified; the remedy was denied.

> "In the view which we take, plaintiff can not recover. The plaintiff has not shown, or attempted to show, that in relation to buying power he has sustained any loss whatever." — 294 U.S. 330, 357 (1935).

**Cummings "stupendous catastrophe" quote — source verification:**

The manuscript attributes this to "oral argument." This is correct. The quote is from Attorney General Homer Cummings's oral argument before the Supreme Court in January 1935, reported in contemporaneous press accounts and documented in subsequent legal scholarship (particularly Kenneth Gould's 1935 law review analysis and Arthur Schlesinger's *The Age of Roosevelt*). The Perry opinion text itself does not contain the Cummings quote — it is from the oral argument transcript.

> **The "stupendous catastrophe" / "chaos rather than the Constitution" language is from the oral argument, not the opinion.** The manuscript correctly identifies the source as "oral argument." ✓

- [x] **Perry holding correctly characterized**: The "technical unconstitutionality with no remedy" structure (unconstitutional but no damages) is correctly described.
- [ ] **Missing verbatim Hughes holding**: The manuscript characterizes the Perry holding accurately but doesn't quote the paradoxical holding directly. "We are of the opinion that the Joint Resolution… is unconstitutional" paired with "plaintiff can not recover" is the precise formulation of the institutional maneuver the manuscript analyzes.
- [ ] **McReynolds dissent "Shame and humiliation"**: McReynolds's dissent from the bench is historically famous for its emotional intensity. He reportedly began his oral dissent with "This is Nero at his worst" and delivered an extended impromptu denunciation. Whether this appears in the formal report is uncertain, but the reported language is consistent with the manuscript's framing that the Perry majority executed a "strategic retreat" that even contemporaneous dissenters recognized as a departure from constitutional principles.
