# Relf v. Weinberger, 372 F. Supp. 1196 (D.D.C. 1974)

**Court:** U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia  
**Citation:** 372 F. Supp. 1196 (D.D.C. 1974)  
**Topic:** Reproductive Coercion / Involuntary Sterilization / 13th Amendment / Equal Protection  
**Corpus Note:** Lower federal court case (not SCOTUS). No primary PDF available in Internet Archive SCOTUS collection. Summary based on published opinion.

---

## Background

Minnie Lee Relf (aged 14) and Mary Alice Relf (aged 12), Black sisters from Montgomery, Alabama, were taken by their mother (who could not read) to a clinic where they were subjected to involuntary sterilization under a federally-funded family planning program administered through the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare (HEW). Their mother believed she was signing consent for birth-control shots, not permanent sterilization. The procedure was performed without meaningful informed consent.

The Southern Poverty Law Center, representing the Relf family, filed suit against Caspar Weinberger, then-Secretary of HEW.

## Holding

Judge Gerhard Gesell held that HEW regulations permitting federally-funded sterilizations without adequate informed consent were **unlawful**. The court found:

1. No statute authorized the use of federal funds for sterilization without genuine informed consent.
2. Regulations permitting such funding were beyond the scope of authority delegated to HEW.
3. Coerced sterilization of welfare recipients violated the rights of the persons sterilized.

The court required HEW to promulgate new regulations with stronger informed consent protections.

## Subsequent History

HEW revised its regulations in 1974 and again in 1978 following *Relf*. The 1978 regulations (42 C.F.R. Part 441) required:
- Written informed consent in the patient's native language
- A 30-day waiting period between consent and procedure
- Prohibition on sterilization of persons under 21 or legally incapacitated

Despite these requirements, coerced sterilizations of incarcerated women in California were documented from 1997–2010 (exposed by the Center for Investigative Reporting), and HHS whistleblower reports in 2020 documented unwarranted hysterectomies on ICE-detained women in Georgia — confirming the kernel's persistence under administrative cover.
