# Madrigal v. Quilligan, No. CV 75-2057 (C.D. Cal. 1978)

**Court:** U.S. District Court for the Central District of California  
**Citation:** No. CV 75-2057-JWC (C.D. Cal. June 30, 1978); unreported  
**Topic:** Reproductive Coercion / Informed Consent / 14th Amendment / Discriminatory Sterilization  
**Corpus Note:** Lower federal court case (not SCOTUS). No primary PDF available in Internet Archive SCOTUS collection. The case was unreported (no published F. Supp. citation); summary based on court documents, the CARECEN legal history, and historian Elena Gutiérrez's research.

---

## Background

Ten Mexican-American women filed suit against Dr. Edward James Quilligan, chief of obstetrics at Los Angeles County-USC Medical Center, alleging that they had been coerced or deceived into consenting to sterilization while in labor or immediately after delivery — a period of extreme physical and psychological vulnerability. Plaintiffs included Dolores Madrigal and nine other women. Several did not speak English; consent forms were presented in English and signed during labor under anesthesia or while in severe pain.

The Center for Constitutional Rights and the Chicana feminist organization MALDEF represented the plaintiffs.

## Holding

Judge Jesse Curtis ruled **against the plaintiffs**, finding that the sterilizations were not the result of intentional racial discrimination. He accepted the hospital's characterization of language barriers and time pressures as explanations for the consent failures, and declined to find a systematic policy of coerced sterilization.

Despite the loss, the case produced:
1. California's 1975 Informed Consent Law (requiring written consent in the patient's primary language, 72-hour waiting period for voluntary sterilization)
2. Federal regulations requiring an interpreter to be present for all sterilization consents in federally-funded programs
3. A documented record of institutional coercion available to historians and advocates

## Subsequent History

The attorney for the plaintiffs, Antonia Hernández, went on to lead MALDEF. The case is documented in Elena Gutiérrez's *Fertile Matters: The Politics of Mexican-Origin Women's Reproduction* (2008). The doctor's perspective was published in a 1977 law review article defending his practices. Coerced sterilizations of Latina women in ICE detention facilities were documented in 2020 (Project South whistleblower report), confirming the kernel's continuity across federal administrative contexts.
