Questions  Presented

[a]  Does  the  Statute  of  Limitations  bar  the  case  of
plaintiff  railroad  employee  who  turned  a  defective  railroad
switch  one  evening  and  felt  a  pulling  sensation  in his  back  but
did not  know  that  he was  injured  and  finished  his shift  that day,
and  it was  not  until  after  12  midnight  or  the  next  day  that  he
first  found  out  that  the pulling  sensation  had  developed  into  an
injury  and  suit  was  commenced  within  three  years  after  the
injury  was  known  or  three  years  and  one  day  after  he  turned
the switch?

[b]  Does  the  District  Court  have  the  right  to  disregard
appellant’s  constitutional  right  to  a  jury  trial  and  on  summary
judgment  disregard  the sworn  contention  of appellant  and  make
a  finding  that  appellant  sustained  his  injuries  at  the  time  he
pulled  the  switch?

the  right  to  disregard  the  applicable  decisions  of this Court  such
as  Urie  vs.  Thompson,  337  US.  163,  69  S.Ct.  1018,  93  L.Ed.

Although  the  complaint  fails  to  set  forth  the  statutory
basis  for  this  court’s jurisdiction,  as  required  by F.R.Civ.P.  S(a),
it  appears  to  be  founded  on  the  Federal  Employers  Liability
Act,  45  U.S.C.  851  et  sec.,  since  it is brought  by a railroad
employee  against  his  employer  on  the  basis  of  alleged
negligence.

The  complaint  in  the  action  was  filed  on  November  2,
1971.  The  complaint  recites  that  plaintiff  was  in  the  act  of
operating  a  switch  on  or  about  November  1,  1968  “and  in
course  of his  employment,  he was  caused  to  sustain  serious  and
severe  personal  injuries.”  paragraph  Fifteenth.  The  accident
report,  presented  at  8:30  p.m.  on  November  1, 1968  and  that
plaintiff described  the  “Nature  of Injury”  as  “lower  back  pains”
and  the  cause  as  “switch  difficult  to  throw.”  The  Opposing
papers  recite  that  plaintiff reported  the incident  to  his superiors
as  a means  of notifying  them  that  the switch  was  defective  “and
also  to  take  into  account  the  possibility  that  this  incident may
develop  into  an  injury  manifesting  itself  sometime  after  the
occurrence  of this particular  incident.”  Plaintiff  remained  at the
yard  until  the  completion  of  his  shift,  was  taken  home  by a
fellow  employee,  and  then  was  examined  as  an  emergency
patient  in Good  Samaritan  Hospital  in the  early  morning hours
of November  2, 1968  and  sent  home  with  a diagnosis  of ‘“‘pulled
muscle,”

No  action  shall  be  maintained  under  this  chapter
unless  commenced  within  three  years  from  the  day  the
cause  of action  accrued.

A cause  of action  accrues  at  the time  of the injury.  Rodzek
v.  N.Y.  Central  R.  R.  Co.,  169  F.  Supp.  803,  805  (E.D.  Mich.
1959).  In  connection  with  an  injury  on a railroad,  the  cause  of
